I started solidcore six months ago. For the uninitiated, it's a form of reformer pilates - a workout where you do very slow movements on a sliding machine with springs that add resistance.

I love it - but not for the reasons I expected.

I've been focused on building muscle and improving my aerobic fitness for the past ~4 years. I figured solidcore would help with that, with a focus on abs. Honestly, I figured I might get a six pack.

But it did something better (and still no six pack - it's true that's made in the kitchen).

After a few months, I noticed big changes in my day-to-day life. Not in the big, visible ways that lifting helps. In the little things. Standing up, sitting down, walking, carrying things, climbing stairs. I have more control over these movements, and more stamina too. My posture has also noticeably improved.

When I started looking into why, I learned that solidcore's slow, sustained movements were targeting something I'd never intentionally trained directly: **muscular endurance**. This post is about what that is and why it matters.

## Muscular endurance - the importance of slow twitch muscle fibers

Before solidcore, I thought about fitness in two categories: lifting weights and cardio. Weights to build muscle, cardio for endurance (I wasn't sure where yoga fit - flexibility with some cardio?).

Solidcore introduced me to a new category: muscular endurance. And it led me to the difference between "slow twitch" and "fast twitch" muscle fibers.

Muscular endurance is how long your muscles can sustain effort before they fatigue. This is different from strength - how much force your muscles can produce, how much weight you can lift. And it's different from aerobic fitness, which is about your heart and lungs.

Understanding why led me to learn about muscle fibers.

Our muscles aren't uniform - they're made of two types of fibers, and different exercises target each.

**Fast twitch fibers** are what most people picture when they think about muscle. They generate a lot of force quickly and grow visibly when you train with heavy weights. But they fatigue quickly.

**Slow twitch fibers** are the opposite. They don't produce as much force, but they resist fatigue and work all day. They hold your posture together, keep you stable when you walk, and let you carry something heavy up a flight of stairs.

Our brains are wired to recruit slow twitch fibers first because they cost less energy; we only recruit fast twitch fibers when we absolutely have to. Slow twitch fibers are the engine behind muscular endurance, and they do the bulk of the work in your daily life.

Training slow twitch fibers doesn't result in big *visual* changes. They won't make you noticeably bigger (there's some evidence they make muscles denser and more defined, but it's subtle). But it makes a big difference in daily life.

## How to train muscular endurance

The key to training slow twitch fibers is **time under tension** - keeping your muscles loaded for extended periods at low enough intensity that slow twitch fibers do the work instead of fast twitch taking over.

In practice, this means: slow movements, no momentum and no rest at any point in the rep. You're not trying to lift as much as possible. You're trying to keep the muscle working for as long as possible.

Any exercise that follows these principles will build muscular endurance. Slow-tempo bodyweight training, certain styles of yoga where you hold poses for extended periods, barre classes, etc.

## The goal: stage two muscle failure, or "the shakes"

When you keep a muscle under tension long enough, it fails in stages.

The first stage is burning - that familiar sensation when your muscles start to fatigue. Holding a plank or wall sit gives that feeling.

The second stage is the shaking. That's your slow twitch fibers breaking down. The micro-tears at this stage trigger the muscle to repair and rebuild stronger and more fatigue-resistant.

It's critical to go slow because if you move quickly or use momentum, you recruit fast twitch fibers and you'll hit a different kind of failure.

## Solidcore as an example

Solidcore is designed to get to stage two muscle failure. Every movement is slow; they recommend at least four seconds on each side, often more. The range of motion is often small - with no bounding or explosive phase.

The machine itself makes this particularly effective. The springs provide constant resistance through the entire range of motion. The sliding carriage means there's no point where your muscles get a break.

## The ripple effects

My cardio also improved. When you run, slow twitch fibers are doing most of the sustained work. I hadn't changed my running routine at all - my runs just started feeling easier.

And muscular endurance training also helps in ways that aren't as obvious. Slow twitch fibers stabilize bones and joints during sustained activity, so strengthening them means better joint support and lower injury risk over time. As we age, our bodies increasingly rely on slow twitch fibers because fast twitch fibers degenerate more quickly.

## The takeaway

Consider focusing on muscular endurance! It was the missing piece of my fitness routine. It has changed how I feel day-to-day.
