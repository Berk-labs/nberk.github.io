This is a list of things I've learned. Or, more accurately, things I try to remember.

This is a living document. I add, remove, and edit as my views evolve.

## Uniqueness

* The ultimate goal is to craft a life that's tailored *to you*.
* One of life's great challenges is that nobody else -- not even those that know you well -- can do the work of figuring out what's right for you.
  * Don't substitute someone else's goals for your own. It can be *extremely* tempting to do so.
  * And that often means rejecting advice, even from those who have your best interests at heart.
  * And, to make it even more challenging, (1) we are often bad at figuring out what we want and (2) our wants change, so it's a moving target.
* The key is experimentation. Try things. Figure out what works.
* Beware of "hero worship." To me, this takes the form of thinking that there's someone who knows what to do in every situation and I'm trying to figure out what they would do (*i.e.*, constantly thinking "what would X do in this situation").
  * This can be a famous person or someone close to us (friend, boss, etc.) It's okay to consider the perspective of those we admire, but, when done too much, it can cause us to lose touch with our intuition, which is frustrating and hurts us in the long run.
  * Also, the premise is, of course, not true: nobody knows what is best for you in your circumstance.
* Also, "one size fits all" solutions are rare.
* Be wary of thinking "I'm not the kind of person who...". We like to comfort ourselves by thinking that we're not *capable* of acting certain ways, but we all act differently in different circumstances. You're capable of more than you think -- both good and bad. Be aware of that.

## Change

* Changing patterns/habits (mental and physical) takes time and effort.
* There are very few pieces of wisdom that you can read once (or even many times) and have it instantly change the way you think or operate. "Aha!" moments happen, but they're rare. It typically takes a lot of contemplation and (often painful) reflection to enact real change.
* It can take a long time to notice the (hopefully positive) effects of changes. Changes, especially at the beginning, require a leap of faith that the effects will be worth it.
* It's best to focus on a few changes at a time.
  * Trying to change a lot at once is daunting and ultimately counterproductive because the lack of change in any area makes you want to give up on all of them.
* When you're trying to change something about yourself, it's incredibly helpful to do so because you *like* yourself (not because you *don't* like yourself). Things are a lot easier when they come from a place of self-compassion than when they come from a place of self-hatred.
* Think as much about what you want to remove from your life as you do about what you want to add. This includes patterns, people, and physical things.

## Important Skills

Many of these are cliche, but they're very true. I call these skills because they can be *developed*.

* **Live in the moment.**
  * We should default to being in the present (as opposed to thinking about the past or the future). This is a real skill -- it can be improved, and it starts with being aware of how often our mind is drifting. I think I'll be working on this forever.
  * This idea is at the heart of so much wisdom and philosophy (there are so many books essentially dedicated to repeating the idea).
* **Don't define yourself by what others think of you.**
  * And -- just for what it's worth, we are terrible at guessing what others are thinking about us -- so most of the time we're spending time worrying about thoughts nobody is having! And most of the time people are thinking about themselves (and not us).
  * This is a tough mindset to adopt because it's objectively true that our reputation matters a lot (in our personal lives and in business) -- so we can fool ourselves into thinking that other peoples' opinion of us is the most important thing.
  * When thinking about "managing your reputation," focus on *acting in a way that you are proud of* (which is, of course, the only thing you can really control). And, similarly, if you want to be seen as being good at a certain thing, *just focus on being good at that thing* -- if you are, other people will eventually notice.
  * Importantly, that may mean that you are less generally appealing. But that's okay -- *you will be appealing to the people who matter*.
  * In all cases, try to judge yourself honestly (you can seek out the opinions of those you trust to help you get a sense of where you are -- but do not blindly accept their opinion). Going through life letting others' opinion of your skills/worth/traits define your worth is like building your foundation on quicksand -- it'll never provide stability.
* **Being happy for others.**
  * There can often be a sick impulse to want others to be unhappy (particularly if you're unhappy). Attempt to genuinely wish others well.
* **Accepting uncertainty / learning to accept what we cannot control.**
  * So much suffering in life is related to our natural hatred for uncertainty and, relatedly, our desire for control -- but life is full of uncertainty and, ultimately, very little is in our control (which is why it's important to focus hard on the things that are within our control).
  * Worrying is a prime example of this -- we do so because we hate not knowing what is going to happen (uncertainty) and worrying gives us the illusion that we are somehow able to exert control over the situation.
  * Finding peace in life requires figuring out a way to deal with how much uncertainty there is and how little we can control it.
* **Resilience.**
  * Learning to pick yourself up and get back out there when things don't go your way.
  * I think part of this is accepting that bouncing back can take longer than we want. Learning to be okay in that rebound is important.
* **Changing your mind (and risking being seen as "inconsistent").**
  * Beware of our desire to be (and to be seen as) someone who has consistent beliefs and interests -- it can trap you into continuing to believe things that are not true or fake interest in things we are no longer interested in.
  * We can and should change our mind, probably a lot more often than we do.
  * The social pressures here can be strong -- our relationships and role in social groups are often based on shared beliefs/interests, or we think that other people would think poorly of us if we changed our beliefs (particularly if it's to one they do not approve of). Be aware of this and be willing to accept the pain of the change to live more authentically.
* **Resting.**
  * It can seem odd to see "rest" as a skill (and, once you do, it can feel like a "productivity hack," which really destroys the point) -- but I've come to believe it is.
  * Finding (healthy) ways to decompress and really just enjoy life is critical to a fulfilling life.
* **Appreciating what is good about your life.**
  *  For people who are obsessed with improvement, this can be difficult and can feel like it can lead to complacency (if you're happy with what you have, won't you start resting and lose it?). But if you don't build the habit of appreciating what you have, life will pass you by while you're in an endless state of wanting. Learn to both appreciate what you have and aspire to improve even more -- it's a tricky balance, but one that is important to be conscious of.
* **Being still.**
  * The point is not to be still more (though we probably should be) -- the point is to have the ability to be still. So we can tap into that when we need it.
* **Being in tune with what we want.**
  * This is easier for some than others.
  * There is a lot of pressure in the world to give other people what they want. And that can be a good instinct. But it's also easy to get lost in what other people want -- and lose our connection with what we want.
  * In any decision/negotiation (even something as small as "where should we eat?"), I've found it useful to start by asking: what do I want? Or: in an ideal world, what would I want to happen? It's simple but powerful. It may be that you don't care that much -- or maybe you do?
* **Setting boundaries.**
  * Step one is having a clear sense internally about what you want and don't want -- or, more specifically, what you won't tolerate (again, see: being in tune with what we want).
  * Boundaries apply to a very wide range of things -- what you want to discuss, eat, etc.
  * Setting boundaries means facing the fear that the other person will reject you. And they might. But the people you want in your life will honor your boundaries.

## Hard Questions

* **What can/should I change about myself? What should I just accept?**
  * On one hand, I think there are a lot more things that we can change about ourselves than we think (this includes many of our mental patterns and even physical things like the way we move). We should approach statements like "this is just how I am and I can't change that" with skepticism; sometimes that's true, but often it isn't.
  * On the other hand, there are clearly things about ourselves that we can't change -- our learning styles and interests come to mind.
  * Again, no clear answer here; this takes self-reflection.
* **When should I push myself? When should I give myself a break?**
  * People fall on different ends of the spectrum in terms of what they struggle with -- some struggle to relax when they need to and some struggle to push themselves when they should.
  * We fall on different ends of the spectrum at different times in our lives (often at different times in the day).
  * It's important to take breaks, both throughout the day and for longer periods in life.
  * But when you get that wave of momentum, ride it.
* **When should I quit? When should I keep going?**
  * Part of the key is to know what your highest-level goals are (*e.g.*, personal freedom, friendships) and to stick to those but be willing to swap out the paths to get there. Still, really there are no easy answers to this one.
  * It's not always applicable, but when you're thinking more about trying to salvage all the time/energy/resources you've spent -- and less about how promising you think the future is, quit.
* **When should I consume/listen more? When should I create/speak more?**
  * It is important to think about the balance between consumption and creation. There is no ideal balance all the time, but it can be important to realize when it's time to switch from one to the other.
  * For me, it's particularly important for me to push myself to switch into "creation" -- because I can consume a lot.

## Getting (Important) Things Done

* Things take way longer than you want or anticipate; this is true of individual tasks and for longer-term projects. Bill Gates said that "most people overestimate what they can do in one year and underestimate what they can do in ten years" -- I think this is very true and powerful.
* When you're trying to accomplish big things (which often takes a long time), get used to relying on your intuition.
  * A lot of big projects involve "long feedback loops" -- where, realistically, it takes a long time to get feedback on whether you're on the right track or not. And you can waste a lot of energy trying to decipher vague, early signals.
  * The reality is that often we can't rely on external signals to tell us whether we're on track (though we should, of course, be alert to signs that we are/aren't) -- we have to trust our gut.
* Thinking about things in life as experiments makes things a lot more fun and interesting and creates an environment in which we are willing to try new things because the stakes seem lower. There are very few things you can't pivot away from.
* Spend way more time thinking. Just sit for an hour and think about what's on your mind, whether it's personal or professional. Don't be afraid to be alone with your thoughts.
* Ideas are a dime a dozen; it's execution that matters (and that's often a question of persistence).
  * Caveat: great ideas are rare, but it usually takes experimentation (*i.e.*, some sort of execution) to uncover them -- so still, execution is key.
* Don't be proud of being busy -- or envious of those who are. Busyness is extremely overrated and often confused with importance.
* Establishing a routine is very steadying -- and very good when you're trying to get things done. It may not mean doing the same thing at the same time every day, but figuring out the things you will do regularly and at what general intervals.
* When working on something that requires problem solving, there is no substitute for sleeping on it (or, again, at least taking a long break). Sometimes we have to finish things in a single day or sitting but, wherever possible, we benefit from time away.
* Careers now, more than ever, are going to involve a lot of change. We are likely going to live longer and the old model of "school, work, retirement" is not going to work; we are going to move between phases more regularly. Many more people are going to have more significant career changes in their lives. Learning to accept and navigate those changes is going to be critical not only for "success," but also for our mental health.
* It is, in general, better to have a bias towards action. But our desire to constantly do/produce *something* can be a big impediment to figuring out what the right or most important thing to do is.
  * In a typical job, if you told your boss you were going to sit or think all day, they would be skeptical -- but this is often the best thing you can do.
  * This problem has a lot of sources, including (i) our desire to see tangible progress (you don't immediately produce anything from thinking); (ii) the discomfort of being still for that long.
    * We think doing something is better than nothing but this often isn't true and misunderstands that sitting there and thinking isn't "nothing."
* The idea of "paying your dues" (*i.e.*, doing things you don't want to in service of some future state) only makes sense when you are working towards something you want. Really consider whether you're moving in the right direction.
* It's so important to learn to separate your inherent sense of self-worth from your professional self-worth. You have worth, and are deserving of love and respect, just by existing. It is not conditional. If there are people in your life who don't get that, get rid of them (to the extent you can).
  * Also, somewhat counterintuitively, the more you learn your inherent self-worth, the freer you feel to try bolder things professionally -- because you know you can handle it if you fail.

## Relationships

* Maybe the most obvious thing: friendships are very important. Spending time with people you enjoy is probably the greatest joy in life. A lot of my life is in service of having the time, money, and strong relationships to make this possible.
* Meeting new people -- or just making casual conversation -- is in large part the art of finding commonality -- finding topics you both are interested in, experiences you both have had. It can be very specific, or a very general human experience (this is why the weather is such an easy one -- we all have some thoughts on the weather). Over time, as you experience things together, those experiences become part of the commonality -- this is how you get lifelong friendships.
* To develop a deeper friendship, someone has to make a move -- you can be that person. Go to dinner. Go do an activity. It really is like dating. Sometimes it results in something, sometimes it doesn't.
* If you appreciate someone, tell them. Be specific.
* Everyone is responsible for their own happiness (except dependent children). Look out for signs that you feel responsible for someone else's happiness -- or that you feel someone else is responsible for your happiness. Neither should be true.
  * This is my basic understanding of "co-dependence." I think some people think of "co-dependence" as relying on each other to do certain things (*e.g.*, sharing household chores) -- but I think it's much more of an emotional state -- a *feeling* that someone other than you is ultimately responsible for your happiness.
* Maintaining friendships takes work (especially when you feel you're at a low point and you'd rather just be alone), but it is the best investment you can make.
* But, don't be afraid to end friendships, or to adjust the amount of time you spend with someone. If you really listen to how you're feeling, you will know when you're dreading the next time you see them or that next call you have scheduled. Adjust accordingly.
* Even for lifelong friends, there will be periods of more frequent contact and periods when you have space. Relationships change. It's okay.
* You really don't need to respond to messages from friends right away. If they're real friends, they'll be okay if you take time to respond.
* Relationships should be a two-way street. If you feel yourself giving a lot more than you're receiving, it may not be healthy. That said, sometimes the relationship will be more "one-way" (*e.g.*, when someone is going through something difficult).
* We make a few big decisions in life, and who we spend the most time with day-to-day is among them. For most people (including me) that's their partner/spouse. It really goes without saying that picking someone who brings you joy and who supports you is one of the best things life has to offer. Conflict resolution is a key skill. It's also important to leave room for change and growth.
* The line between friend and colleague (*i.e.*, "work" and "personal") gets fuzzier over time. Colleagues become friends and you can have work-related interactions with your friends.

## Health

* Health -- both mental and physical -- is the foundation of everything. If these two things are not in order, it is extremely difficult to build a stable (not to mention a long) life.
* Proper sleep hygiene is one of the best things you can do for yourself. Don't bring your phone into your bedroom; get up/go to bed at the same time as much as possible; get enough sleep (usually about getting to bed on time). The science on this is so clear -- if our sleep isn't right, it's hard to get much else done.
* Tinker with your diet to find what works for you (this takes time and work -- but is well worth the investment).
  * This is one of the many things in life where, although there is general advice (*e.g.*, Michael Pollan's advice to "eat food, not a lot, mostly plants," seems like good advice) -- the real game is to find what works *for you* (and your family). Nobody can tell you that, and you probably can't figure it out without a little experimentation.
  * That said, you can achieve a lot by significantly reducing processed carbs and sugar.

## Information/News Consumption

* Be intentional about the news/information you consume.
  * In a world of essentially limitless information on every subject, it's important to be intentional about what we consume (and when).
* Strongly consider how you can set boundaries around the amount of content you consume. This can be time based, but that seems hard to enforce. Using an RSS Reader (which basically creates a checklist of articles from particular sources) is an excellent way to do this.
* The large majority of information you come across is not useful or even interesting -- it won't matter in a month. Take it all with a grain of salt.
* Knowing more about all the suffering in the world (and there is a lot) -- and/or thinking about it more often -- doesn't make you a better person. Doing something to fix it does. Don't fool yourself into thinking you're helping the planet by reading more news -- the marginal value of each additional article is a lot lower than you think.
  * Realize that you often enjoy the feeling of outrage and that it's pure indulgence -- it's not helping the world.
* If you really want to make a difference, finding a way to spend an hour a week volunteering in your local community -- or by upping your donation to a great charity -- are far better ways to go about it.
* Say no to a lot more content and sources. Unsubscribe aggressively. Don't finish an article that's boring you.
* Seek out fun/niche content. I follow blogs about new junk/fast food (even though I don't eat a ton of either) and I follow a few theme park blogs because it's light. It's also fun to see people who are passionate about a subject. I have recently rediscovered the joy of all the weird, fun stuff the internet has to offer (it feels like the mid 2000s again).
* Read more books, less about current events (how many times has this been said -- but it's so true).
  * I've found that one of the keys to making this happen is to make reading/listening to a book as easy as reading the news. For me, this means keeping my kindle nearby. It is, of course, also important to find the books you're interested in reading (often this is different from the books you *want* to be interested in reading -- or the ones that your friends recommended).
* If you're reading a book to really learn something from it (either on a particular subject or in a self-help way) -- it's very difficult to really get anything out of it without taking notes or otherwise interacting with the book (*e.g.*, highlighting). Really internalizing the information takes time and work.
* Try audiobooks. They may work for you, they may not. I've tried it with mixed results.
* Also, not that movies need a plug, but movies are great. Go to the movies.

## General / Misc

* One of the most mentally destructive ideas is that we'll be happy once a certain set of conditions are true (*e.g.*, once we make a certain amount of money, get a certain promotion, get married, move to a particular city, retire). You get to that next state, and the contentment vanishes nearly immediately.
* Surrounding yourself with people who are better than you are is one of the best things you can do, but it isn't easy day-to-day. It can lead to feelings of inadequacy and frustration. But there are moments when you step back and you realize how much you have improved because of spending time with these types of people and (like most exercises of delayed gratification), it's so worth it.
* It's impossible to know what's good news and what's bad news in the moment -- what feels like a failure may be the stepping stone to something important and what feels like a success may have been something that kept you from something better, or something that has the seeds of failure.
* We underestimate the importance of *implicit* memory, which (very generally speaking) is our memory of how to do things. When we think of "having a good memory," we typically think about "explicit memory," which is our ability to recall specific pieces of information. We often don't give ourselves enough credit for building implicit memory because it accrues slowly over time -- but it is one of our biggest assets.
* Forcing yourself to write things down is one of the most powerful tools to clarify your thinking and understanding (hence this list).
* When you're thinking about what kind of life you want to live, think about your 100th Birthday Party -- what would you want people to say about you?
* If you're seeking to really learn about a topic, really consider what your current opinion is and how willing you are to change it. The vast majority of the time when we "seek out additional information" it is just to confirm what we already believe.
* The lesson from the tortoise and the hare is so true -- consistency beats sporadic bursts.
* The world is driven far more by envy than by greed -- and both are extremely toxic traits. So much suffering comes from comparing ourselves to others. I honestly haven't figured out a way to get rid of envy, but being aware of it is a first step.
* It's amazing how much we can learn about ourselves by being more in tune with our body (again, this is something that's easy to read but takes a lot of time to cultivate). Pause right now; do a body scan -- what are you really feeling? Our bodies tell us so much about what is healthy for us, what we want and don't want, etc.
* Happiness is knowing that whatever happens, you will figure it out.
* Go outside. Even for a few minutes. You won't regret it.
* People go through peaks and valleys in their lives -- to expect that you're always going to be at a peak, or that you're never going to be in a valley when someone else is at a peak, is a recipe for misery (again, it's very hard to tell what's a peak or a valley in the moment).
  * I love this Orson Welles quote: *"If you want a happy ending, that depends, of course, on where you stop your story."* -- it really captures that life is cyclical.
* We over-index on trying to model ourselves on public figures or people in positions of leadership; these positions don't mean they're happy, and it may actually make it less likely that they are because they spend so much energy keeping up appearances.
  * There are many people out there quietly living extremely happy lives (no matter how much money they have) -- but they're harder to emulate because we don't hear from them. Be on the lookout for glimpses of the lives of people who are quietly living full lives.
* Almost everyone has some issue in their personal life that they're dealing with at any particular moment -- a conflict with a relative, a parent with a health issue, a child struggling with addiction. It's important to view interactions through that lens and to be compassionate. These experiences can feel isolating but, because they're part of being human, they ultimately connect us.
* That thing that you're currently really worried about and are convinced that if it happens, your life will be unsalvageable -- it's probably not going to happen and, if it does, you're going to figure it out.
* Take the time to get a rough sense of your spending patterns. Know how much you spend in an average month and know what you could get it down to if you needed to. So much financial anxiety (even among people with plenty of money) comes from not really knowing how much they need -- rather than from an actual lack of money (though, of course, there can be real financial anxiety about not having enough money).
* Realize that, even if you're close with your sibling(s), each of you had a different experience and it's important to think about things from your own perspective.
* (Especially if you struggle with ADHD or ADHD-like symptoms) focus on how you can control your environment. For example, I carefully curate which notifications I get and I often have to physically put my phone in my other room (I know there's a lot written on this topic of managing distractions). This one takes some tinkering.
* You are not your thoughts -- "you" (a more elusive concept than it appears) are hearing your thoughts. You don't have to -- and most of the time shouldn't -- engage.
* Growth is not learning how to live without negative emotions; it is learning to live with them.
* Being pessimistic makes people feel and sound smart -- it's easy to come up with reasons why things won't work or something wasn't good. And it's important to assess things realistically. But the future is created by optimists.
* People love to talk about themselves (there are exceptions, but they're rare). If you can figure out a way to be interested in them, you're going to seem charismatic. Everyone wants to feel interesting and acknowledged.
